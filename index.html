01 — Problem Statement

A city built too dense for the trees it needs.

Kigali's rapid urbanisation has outpaced the soil, space, and time that conventional greening requires — leaving a measurable, worsening gap in air quality across its busiest districts.

Kigali's average PM2.5 concentration was recorded at 52 µg/m³ between March 2017 and July 2018 — more than ten times the World Health Organization's annual guideline of 5 µg/m³. The sources are structural, not seasonal: vehicle exhaust concentrated around terminals like Nyabugogo, biomass combustion in low-income settlements, and construction dust from a city still building outward.

Traditional tree planting cannot close this gap fast enough. Kigali's urban core sits on compacted soils with limited available land, and the maintenance cost of establishing mature canopy at scale is prohibitive within the timeframe public health requires. The same density that causes the pollution also removes the conventional remedy for it.

Left unaddressed, the World Health Organization estimates air pollution contributes to over 4 million premature deaths globally each year — with developing cities, lacking Kigali's scale of green infrastructure, absorbing a disproportionate share of that burden.

02 — Precedent & Literature

This is not unproven. It has already worked once.

The technology Kigali would deploy already has an operating reference: a 600-litre photobioreactor on a public street in Belgrade, monitored for over two years by a national development agency.

Belgrade, Serbia

The LIQUID3 prototype, coordinated by UNDP Serbia

Developed with the University of Belgrade, LIQUID3 integrates native microalgae in a solar-powered tank to absorb CO₂ and produce oxygen — installed directly on a public street rather than a lab, to test real urban conditions from day one.

10–50× carbon sequestration efficiency confirmed

Published in the International Journal of Plant and Environment, this study found microalgae in photobioreactors achieve carbon sequestration rates 10 to 50 times that of terrestrial trees per unit area, and documented secondary value in biomass-derived fertilisers and biofuels.

Villalba et al., 2023

Climate adaptability identified as the open variable

Urban BioPods research across variable climates flagged temperature control and species selection as the critical levers for performance outside temperate zones — directly motivating this study's species and thermal design choices for Kigali's tropical context.

This study, 2026

Adapting the model to a 20–27°C tropical context

Kigali's climate sits inside the optimal range for thermotolerant strains, meaning the Belgrade design's temperature-control burden is structurally lower here — the central engineering opportunity this feasibility study sets out to confirm.

04 — Operating Sequence

From polluted air to clean exhaust, in eight steps.

The process runs continuously and unattended: each stage below feeds directly into the next, closing the loop from energy capture to biomass output.

1
Solar energy capture
Component B — Solar Panel
The roof-mounted solar panel absorbs sunlight and converts it to electricity, powering every electrical subsystem: the LED grow-light arrays, the automated air pump, the multi-sensor module, the IoT gateway, and the public display. The unit is fully off-grid capable.
2
Light delivery to the algae
Component C — LED Arrays
Dual lateral LED arrays at 450nm blue and 680nm red supply supplemental light during night-time or low-sunlight periods, so photosynthesis continues 24 hours a day regardless of cloud cover.
3
Polluted air intake & CO₂ injection
Component E — Air Pump
The automated air pump draws ambient polluted air — rich in CO₂, vehicle exhaust, and PM2.5 — from the surrounding street, and injects it as fine bubbles into the base of the tank to maximise contact with the culture.
4
Photosynthesis & CO₂ absorption
Component D — Microalgae Culture
Microalgae absorb dissolved CO₂ and use solar/LED energy to drive photosynthesis — CO₂ + H₂O + Light → O₂ + Biomass — at 10 to 50 times the carbon-sequestration efficiency of terrestrial trees per unit area, operating natively within Kigali's 20–27°C range.
5
Nutrient feed & culture maintenance
Component F — Nutrient System
An automated dosing system supplies a balanced N/P/K solution. The closed-loop water system minimises waste, while pH, temperature, and algal density are continuously measured and adjusted to keep the culture at peak productivity.
6
Oxygen release
O₂ output port
Purified oxygen rises to the top of the tank and exits through the outlet. Each unit outputs approximately 4.2 g O₂/hour — equivalent to 36–80 kg per year, the output of two mature trees — while treating 300–3,000 m³ of air daily.
7
Real-time monitoring & data transmission
Component G — Sensor Module + IoT
Onboard sensors stream CO₂, O₂, PM2.5, temperature, pH, and algae-density readings via a 4G IoT gateway to a central environmental database, and to the public-facing panel mounted on the tank itself.
8
Biomass harvest & circular economy
Component I — Biomass Port
Excess algae biomass is periodically extracted through the harvest port and processed into organic fertiliser for urban agriculture, biofuel feedstock, or protein-rich feed — closing a circular resource loop that generates value beyond air purification alone.

Per square metre, not per tree.

The comparison that matters for Kigali is not "one tank versus one tree" — it is output per unit of scarce urban land. A single Liquid Tree footprint of roughly 1.2 m² is documented as equivalent in oxygen output to two mature trees or 200 m² of lawn, neither of which Kigali's dense core has room to plant. The tank does not replace conventional greening — it operates in the spaces conventional greening structurally cannot reach.

07 — Urban Heat Island Context

The same density that traps pollution also traps heat.

Compact urban cores like Kigali's experience both problems through one shared cause: hard surfaces that replace vegetation. A water-based, photosynthesising system is structurally positioned to address both.

Kigali's urban heat island effect follows the pattern documented across compact tropical cities: asphalt, concrete, and roofing absorb solar radiation through the day and re-radiate it through the night, while the tree cover that would otherwise provide shade and evaporative cooling has been displaced by the same construction driving the city's growth. The original concept note identifies this directly — compacted soils and limited land availability constrain traditional greening, exacerbating the urban heat island effect and reducing air purification capacity at the same time.

"A liter of water absorbs more heat before changing temperature than almost any common building material — which is precisely what a 600-litre tank holds in the middle of a heat-retaining streetscape."

This is the structural case for treating Liquid Tree Tanks as dual-purpose climate infrastructure, not air-quality infrastructure with a side benefit. The same large thermal mass of water that supports a stable algae culture also resists the rapid heating that asphalt and concrete undergo, while the live evapotranspiration-like process — air drawn in, processed, oxygen released — keeps a continuous exchange running at street level, in exactly the hard-surfaced plazas, bus terminals, and market squares where canopy cover is least available and heat stress is most acute.

Mechanisms at Street Level

Thermal mass buffering

600 litres of water per unit absorbs and releases heat far more gradually than the bare pavement it sits on, moderating the immediate microclimate around each tank.

Continuous air exchange

Unlike static infrastructure, the unit actively draws in and processes ambient air around the clock, rather than simply reflecting or absorbing solar load.

Deployable on hard surfaces

Requiring no soil, the tank can occupy precisely the paved plazas and terminals where canopy cover is structurally impossible and heat retention is highest.

Compounds with pollution reduction

Lower PM2.5 and higher local oxygen concentration are co-located with the cooling effect, rather than requiring separate infrastructure for each.

Scope note: Microclimate cooling is a documented co-benefit pathway of water-based, photosynthesising systems in dense urban contexts. This study has not yet collected site-specific temperature data for Kigali; quantifying the heat-island effect at pilot sites is identified as a measurement priority for the monitoring phase in Section 09.
08 — Multi-Dimensional Benefits

A return measured in three different currencies.

Environmental performance is the headline, but the case for deployment also depends on economic and social value generated alongside it.

Environmental

  • CO₂ absorption and O₂ production at 10–50× tree efficiency per square metre
  • PM2.5 filtration reducing particle load within a 50 m radius of each unit
  • Documented thermal-mass mechanism for urban heat island mitigation
  • GHG reduction concentrated in high-emission transport microenvironments
  • Biomass converts to organic fertiliser, supporting urban agriculture
  • Operates with no soil required — functions where trees biologically cannot

Economic

  • Smart monetisation of limited Kigali public land, much of it currently unused
  • Biomass processing yields high-value fertiliser and biofuel feedstock
  • USB charging and advertising space generate supplemental site revenue
  • Reduced healthcare burden from respiratory and cardiovascular illness
  • Carbon credit potential under Rwanda's NDC framework

Social & Institutional

  • Positions Rwanda as an African leader in biotech-based urban greening
  • Live data displays educate citizens on air quality and climate action
  • Creates urban meeting points that enhance the public realm experience
  • Directly supports SDG 11 (Sustainable Cities) and SDG 13 (Climate Action)
  • Builds local capacity in algae biotechnology and environmental monitoring
09 — Implementation Roadmap

Five phases, analysis to city-wide scale.

Each phase will generate the evidence the next phase depends on.

Q1 2026

GIS Analysis & Design

Pollution-overlay mapping will guide species selection and climate adaptation for Kigali.

Q2 2026

Pilot Deployment

Q3 2026

Monitoring Phase

Baseline and post-installation readings will be compared; biomass yield will be recorded.
